I've been meaning to start writing for a while now. Not because I have everything figured out, but because I have a lot going on in my head and writing has always been how I make sense of it. That's what this blog is going to be: a window into the things I'm thinking about. My journey, the things I'm learning, the questions I can't stop turning over.

Right now, a lot of that is AI. It's hard to avoid when it's moving this fast and touching everything. So that's what I'm writing about first. But this won't always be an AI blog. It's just what's dominating the space in my head at the moment, and it feels like the right place to start.

So here's where I am with it, and how I got here.

---

Here's the thing you should know about where I was when I first started using AI tools: I had just come out of a digital minimalism phase. I'd gotten off Instagram, deleted the news apps, turned off basically every notification on my phone. I was actively trying to use *less* technology. So the fact that I ended up going deep on AI is a little funny in retrospect.

A friend in my industry kept raving about what he was doing with ChatGPT. I resisted for a while, then finally got curious enough to dive in.

---

My first real experiment was creating a persona of Jean-Luc Picard from Star Trek. I just kept asking him questions about the ship, his crewmates, the Federation. I thought it was pretty hilarious. It was. But it wasn't exactly unlocking anything profound.

The real breakthrough came later that year when I started trying to write a book.

I was using ChatGPT to help me keep track of ideas, pressure test plot points, and get feedback on dialogue I wanted to be historically accurate for the time period. What blew me away was how well it handled the way my brain actually works. I could write in pure stream of consciousness, just let everything pour out, and it could capture all of it, organize it, and make it easy for me to curate until it started to look like what I was actually going for.

It was addicting. I spent a lot of nights up until 2am working on that book.

I didn't finish it. My AI use started blooming in other areas, work especially, and my time went there instead. I'll go back to the book someday. Same with the comic book idea I picked up along the way. They were genuinely a lot of fun to work on, and that kind of creative exploration taught me things about how to use these tools that I never would have learned otherwise.

---

What I kept noticing across all of it was that every time I thought I had a handle on what these tools could do, they changed. Or I changed. Or both.

The pace is genuinely hard to describe. It's not just that the models are getting better, though they are. It's that my own sense of what's possible keeps getting revised. Sometimes by the month. Sometimes by the week. There have been stretches where it felt like by the day. That's exciting. Occasionally disorienting. But mostly exciting.

---

I almost always go in with a goal. The thing I don't know is how I'm going to get there. So I just start. I share context, throw out questions, think out loud, flag the things that need more thought. And AI helps me work along the path as I keep iterating. It meets me where I am and moves with me as the thinking gets clearer. That's been the consistent pattern across everything I've worked on, whether it was a novel, a comic book, or an operating model for a product team.

I'm writing this because I want to share some of those learnings. Not because I've cracked some code. But because I keep talking to people who are where I was two years ago, and I remember how much I would have appreciated hearing from someone just a little further along the path.

I'm just trying to figure stuff out, same as everyone else. Two years of trying is worth something, though. At least I think so.
